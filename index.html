The Upgrade Ladder

You bought your ticket. But that's not the end of the story. Many venues allow day-of upgrades, section moves, lounge add-ons, and hospitality access — and almost no one explains it clearly.

01

Start with any ticket

Enter the venue with a standard ticket. Even upper-level or obstructed-view seats can be your starting point.

02

Check venue upgrade programs

Many arenas and stadiums offer official upgrade desks or kiosks inside the venue. Day-of pricing is often significantly reduced.

03

Monitor your ticketing app

Ticketmaster, AXS, and venue apps may push upgrade offers as the event approaches. These are legitimate, platform-verified moves.

04

Add hospitality access

Some venues sell lounge passes, club-level access, or premium food & beverage packages as standalone add-ons to any ticket.

05

Ask the box office

The simplest path: walk to the box office and ask what's available. Premium inventory that didn't sell is often released at face value on event day.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to the questions planners, fans, and event-goers ask most. Every answer here is TicketSecret Verified.

What are "production hold" tickets and when do they get released?

Production hold tickets are seats reserved by the production team, promoter, or venue for operational reasons — such as sightline adjustments, staging changes, or VIP accommodations. These tickets are often released 48 to 72 hours before the event once the production layout is finalized.

They frequently appear on platforms like Ticketmaster as a sudden batch of available seats after a show was listed as "sold out." Checking back close to the event date is one of the most reliable ways to find these late-release tickets.

What are the hidden ticket types most people don't know exist?

Hidden ticket types include sponsor-held tickets (reserved by corporate sponsors like credit card companies), artist-held tickets (set aside for the performer's guests and fan clubs), venue-held tickets (reserved for premium members or suite holders), promoter-held tickets, radio and media allocations, casino host tickets, hotel-bundled packages, production holds, and membership-only allocations.

These categories are not typically shown on public seating maps and follow their own release schedules.

How do sponsor-held and promoter-held tickets work?

Sponsor-held tickets are allocated to corporate sponsors (such as Amex, Citi, or beverage brands) as part of their sponsorship agreement. These are often released through exclusive presales or card-member portals.

Promoter-held tickets are reserved by the event promoter (like Live Nation or AEG) and may be released through their own platforms, loyalty programs, or as last-minute inventory. Both types typically offer premium locations and are released on schedules separate from the general public onsale.

How do credit card presales actually work (Amex, Citi, Chase)?

Credit card presales work through sponsorship agreements between card issuers and event promoters. American Express, Citi, and Chase each have dedicated portals where cardholders can access tickets 24 to 72 hours before the general public.

Amex often partners with major tours for front-of-house seats. Citi provides access through its Entertainment portal. Chase offers presale access via its Sapphire and Freedom card programs. The key is knowing which card sponsors which tour and registering in advance on the corresponding presale platform.

What is the most legitimate way to get VIP tickets?

The most legitimate ways to get VIP tickets include: purchasing official VIP packages directly from the artist's website or authorized sellers, accessing credit card presales with premium seating options, joining venue membership or loyalty programs that include VIP allocations, purchasing hospitality packages through the venue's premium services division, and using verified resale platforms for transferred VIP inventory.

Always verify that VIP perks (early entry, lounge access, merchandise) are explicitly listed before purchasing.

What is "quiet VIP" and who is it best for?

Quiet VIP refers to premium ticket experiences that prioritize comfort, privacy, and convenience over flashy perks. This includes private entrances, dedicated lounges, spacious seating, in-seat service, and early or flexible entry and exit.

Quiet VIP is ideal for executives, older fans, introverts, neurodiverse attendees, and parents — anyone who wants a premium live event experience without crowds, noise, or social pressure. Venue suites, club-level seats, and hospitality lounges are common quiet VIP options.

How do you upgrade concert tickets after you already bought them?

Many venues and platforms offer ticket upgrades through several pathways: using the venue's official upgrade program (many arenas offer day-of upgrades at the box office), through the Ticketmaster or AXS app which may offer upgrade prompts, by contacting the venue's premium services or concierge department directly, or by purchasing a hospitality add-on that includes section access upgrades.

The availability and pricing of upgrades varies by venue, event demand, and timing — day-of upgrades at the box office often offer the best value.

What tickets exist that are not shown on public seating maps?

Tickets not shown on public seating maps include: VIP platform or stage-adjacent areas, production hold seats (released late), venue premium lounges and suites, sponsor-reserved blocks, standing-room-only sections released after sellouts, ADA companion seats released individually, artist guest list allocations, and casino or hotel guest blocks.

These seats exist in the venue layout but are controlled through separate distribution channels and are often not visible on standard ticketing platform maps.

What is the difference between VIP packages and hospitality seats?

VIP packages are typically sold by the artist or tour and bundle a concert ticket with extras like early entry, merchandise, meet-and-greet opportunities, or premium viewing areas.

Hospitality seats are sold by the venue and focus on the venue experience — private suites, club-level lounges, in-seat food and beverage service, and premium parking. VIP packages center on the artist experience; hospitality centers on venue comfort. They occasionally overlap but are sold through different channels with different inclusions.

What are the safest and smartest ways to get tickets after a show sells out?

After a sellout, the safest approaches are: (1) Wait for production hold releases 48–72 hours before the show, (2) Check official resale platforms like Ticketmaster Verified Resale or AXS Official Resale, (3) Monitor for additional dates or venue changes that create new inventory, (4) Check credit card presale portals which may still have unreleased allocations, (5) Contact the venue box office directly for day-of releases, and (6) Look for hospitality or hotel packages that bundle tickets separately from general inventory.

Avoid unverified resellers and social media listings.

What does "hospitality" mean in ticketing?

In ticketing, hospitality refers to premium venue-managed experiences that go beyond a standard seat. This includes private suites, club-level lounges with dedicated food and beverage service, premium parking, private entrances, and in-seat wait service.

Hospitality is sold by the venue (not the artist) and is designed for comfort and convenience. It's often available for purchase even when standard tickets are sold out, as it operates on a separate inventory system.

What are the best credit cards for concert presales?

The top credit cards for concert presales are American Express (particularly Platinum and Gold cards, which get first access to major tour presales), Citi (any Citi card accesses the Citi Entertainment program), Chase (Sapphire Preferred and Reserve for Chase presale access), and Capital One (especially for events at Capital One-branded venues).

The best strategy is to hold cards from multiple issuers, as different cards sponsor different tours. Register on each card's entertainment portal before presales are announced.
